# The Language of Knowledge Institutions
## A Copywriting Style Guide for Noble, Non-Marketing Institutional Voice

---

## Part I: Institution-by-Institution Analysis

---

### 1. WIKIPEDIA / WIKIMEDIA FOUNDATION

**Key Copy Specimens:**

- Tagline: "The free encyclopedia that anyone can edit."
- Jimmy Wales' vision: "Imagine a world in which every single person on the planet is given free access to the sum of all human knowledge. That's what we're doing."
- Wikimedia Foundation mission: "The mission of the Wikimedia Foundation is to empower and engage people around the world to collect and develop educational content under a free license or in the public domain, and to disseminate it effectively and globally."
- Permanence commitment: "The Foundation will make and keep useful information from its projects available on the internet free of charge, in perpetuity."

**Adjectives used:** free, educational, useful, effective, global. Notably absent: innovative, revolutionary, cutting-edge, powerful, amazing, best-in-class.

**Verbs used:** collect, develop, disseminate, empower, engage, make available, keep. These are verbs of stewardship and distribution, not conquest or disruption.

**Sentence structure:** The tagline is a masterpiece of compression -- ten words, no wasted syllable. The mission statement uses a single long sentence with stacked infinitives ("to collect... to develop... to disseminate"). It reads like a charter, not a pitch. Wales' vision statement uses the imperative "Imagine" followed by a simple declarative: "That's what we're doing." No qualifiers.

**Emotional register:** Quiet inevitability. The word "perpetuity" does enormous work. It signals permanence beyond any company's lifecycle. The language assumes the project's importance rather than arguing for it. There is zero defensiveness. Wikipedia does not explain why free knowledge matters -- it treats that as self-evident.

**What they notably do NOT do:**
- Never use superlatives about themselves ("the world's best," "the leading")
- Never reference competitors or alternatives
- Never use urgency language ("now," "today," "don't miss")
- Never describe features or benefits in marketing terms
- Use "free" to mean libre/gratis, not as a sales hook

### 2. arXiv

**Key Copy Specimens:**

- Lead: "arXiv is a curated research-sharing platform open to anyone."
- Identity: "As a pioneer in digital open access, arXiv.org now hosts more than two million scholarly articles in eight subject areas, curated by our strong community of volunteer moderators."
- Values: "openness, collaboration, and scholarship"
- Function list: "article submission, compilation, production, retrieval, search and discovery, web distribution for human readers, and API access for machines, together with content curation and preservation."
- Founding: "arXiv was founded by Paul Ginsparg in 1991."
- Quality note: "Material is not peer-reviewed by arXiv."

**Adjectives used:** curated, open, strong, scholarly. The adjective inventory is remarkably small. arXiv barely uses adjectives at all.

**Verbs used:** hosts, curated, sharing, submission, retrieval, preservation. These are archival and infrastructural verbs -- the language of a repository, not a product.

**Sentence structure:** Short, declarative, factual. "arXiv is..." followed by what it is. Not what it could be, not what it aspires to be. The function description is a plain list -- no verb phrases like "empowers you to" or "lets you easily." It just names the functions as nouns. The founding date is stated as a bare historical fact, like an encyclopedia entry about itself.

**Emotional register:** Academic restraint. There is an almost deliberate anti-charisma. The most emotionally charged phrase is "pioneer in digital open access," and even that reads as a historical fact rather than a boast. The word "strong" modifying "community" is about the most promotional thing on the page.

**What they notably do NOT do:**
- Never describe impact or transformation ("changing how science works")
- Never use testimonials or social proof
- Never quantify growth trajectories or momentum
- State a limitation honestly and directly ("Material is not peer-reviewed")
- The number "two million" is presented as a fact, not an achievement

### 3. INTERNET ARCHIVE

**Key Copy Specimens:**

- Mission: "Universal Access to All Knowledge."
- Self-description: "A non-profit library with a huge mission: to give everyone access to all knowledge, forever."
- Vision: "A library containing all the published works of humankind, free to the public, built to last the ages."
- Identity: "The Internet's Library."
- Tagline: "Digital Library of Free & Borrowable Texts, Movies, Music & Wayback Machine."

**Adjectives used:** universal, free, digital, huge. But the power words here are the totalizing determiners: "all," "every," "everyone." The Internet Archive speaks in absolutes.

**Verbs used:** give, access, built, last. These are ancient, simple verbs. "Built to last the ages" uses the language of architecture and monument-making, not software.

**Sentence structure:** The mission statement is five words. That is not a sentence; it is a declaration -- almost a motto chiseled in stone. The longer descriptions use appositive structures: "A library containing... free to the public, built to last the ages." This is the grammar of inscriptions. Commas separating parallel phrases, building toward a crescendo. "Forever" stands alone at the end of a sentence like a keystone.

**Emotional register:** Civilizational ambition stated with the calm of a librarian. The word "ages" invokes deep time. "All knowledge" invokes totality. "Everyone" invokes universality. Yet the framing is always "library" -- the most humble, civic, trusted institution in human culture. The tension between the cosmic scope and the humble framing is what creates the sense of nobility.

**What they notably do NOT do:**
- Never describe technology stacks or technical innovation
- Never use startup language (disrupt, scale, leverage)
- Frame themselves as a library, not a platform or a tech company
- "Forever" and "the ages" place the project in civilizational time, not product cycles

### 4. CERN

**Key Copy Specimens:**

- Core mission: "At CERN, our work helps to uncover what the universe is made of and how it works."
- Method: "We probe the fundamental structure of particles that make up everything around us. We do so using the world's largest and most complex scientific instruments."
- Founding narrative: "A handful of visionary scientists imagined creating a European atomic physics laboratory."
- Stated mission pillars:
  - "perform world-class research in fundamental physics"
  - "provide a unique range of particle accelerator facilities that enable research at the forefront of human knowledge"
  - "unite people from all over the world to push the frontiers of science and technology, for the benefit of all"
  - "train new generations of physicists, engineers and technicians"
- Physics description: "An accelerator propels charged particles, such as protons or electrons, at high speeds, close to the speed of light."
- Discovery framing: "By studying these collisions, physicists are able to probe the world of the infinitely small."
- Temporal framing: "The energy of the collision is transformed into matter in the form of new particles, the most massive of which existed in the early universe."

**Adjectives used:** fundamental, largest, most complex, world-class, unique, infinitely small, early. These adjectives serve precision, not persuasion. "Fundamental" is used in its literal sense (relating to foundations), not as a marketing intensifier. "Largest" and "most complex" are factual superlatives -- these are literally the biggest instruments ever built.

**Verbs used:** probe, uncover, make up, propels, collide, transform, push, unite, enable. CERN's verbs are active and physical. They describe the actual mechanics of what happens: particles are propelled, they collide, energy is transformed. The verbs carry the drama inherently because the subject matter is dramatic.

**Sentence structure:** CERN uses a signature opening construction: "At CERN, ..." This locative opening establishes place before action. It says: this is a place where things happen. Many sentences follow an observation-method pattern: what we seek to know, then how we seek to know it. The founding narrative uses the word "imagined" -- a single verb that elevates the founders without hagiography.

**Emotional register:** Awe through understatement. The sentence "the most massive of which existed in the early universe" is staggering in its implications but delivered as a factual observation. CERN lets the science carry the weight. "The world of the infinitely small" is poetic but accurate. The phrase "for the benefit of all" appears almost as an aside, a quiet ethical commitment tucked into a list.

**What they notably do NOT do:**
- Never hype their own importance -- they describe what they study and let the reader supply the awe
- Never use business language (ROI, stakeholders, deliverables)
- The word "imagined" in the founding narrative is the only lyrical flourish, and it earns its place
- Factual superlatives only -- "largest" and "most complex" are literally true, not promotional

### 5. PROJECT GUTENBERG

**Key Copy Specimens:**

- Opening: "Project Gutenberg is an online library of more than 75,000 free eBooks."
- Mission: "To encourage the creation and distribution of eBooks."
- Founding: "Michael Hart invented eBooks in 1971."
- Philosophy: "The greatest value created by computers would not be computing, but would be the storage, retrieval, and searching of what was stored in our libraries."
- Permanence: "A decade from now we probably won't have the same operating systems... but Alice in Wonderland, the Bible, Shakespeare... will be with us as long as civilization."
- Accessibility: Etexts should be "made available in the simplest, easiest to use forms available."
- Core belief: "Anything that can be entered into a computer can be reproduced indefinitely."

**Adjectives used:** free, simple, easy, plain, vast. Project Gutenberg's adjective palette is deliberately humble. "Plain Vanilla ASCII" is an anti-style statement -- it rejects ornamentation in both format and language.

**Verbs used:** encourage, create, distribute, store, retrieve, search, reproduce. The mission verb is "encourage" -- not "revolutionize," not "transform," not even "provide." It is a gentle verb. It positions the project as a catalyst, not a savior.

**Sentence structure:** "Michael Hart invented eBooks in 1971." Six words. Subject-verb-object-date. This is a statement of historical fact presented with the confidence of an encyclopedia entry. It does not say "visionary founder" or "pioneering inventor." It just says "invented." The philosophy passages use longer, more reflective sentences -- Hart thinking aloud about civilization and permanence. The contrast between the short declaratives and the philosophical passages creates a texture of practical idealism.

**Emotional register:** Practical utopianism. Hart's voice is that of someone who has thought deeply about civilization but expresses it through the lens of file formats and accessibility. "As long as civilization" is the emotional peak -- and it arrives in a sentence about ASCII encoding. The juxtaposition of the mundane (file formats) with the eternal (civilization) is the source of the project's quiet grandeur.

**What they notably do NOT do:**
- The mission statement is almost comically understated -- "To encourage the creation and distribution of eBooks" for a project that has digitized 75,000+ works of human literature
- Never use the language of innovation or disruption, despite literally having invented the eBook
- "Invented" is stated as fact, not celebrated as achievement
- The philosophy is expressed through concrete examples (Alice in Wonderland, the Bible, Shakespeare) rather than abstractions

## Part II: Cross-Institutional Patterns

### Verbs: What These Institutions DO vs. What They Never Say

**Verbs they use:**
- Stewardship verbs: preserve, maintain, curate, host, keep, store
- Access verbs: share, disseminate, distribute, give, provide, make available
- Knowledge verbs: uncover, probe, discover, study, search, retrieve
- Community verbs: unite, engage, collaborate, encourage, empower
- Building verbs: create, build, develop, collect, train

**Verbs they never use:**
- Disruption verbs: disrupt, transform, revolutionize, reimagine, reinvent
- Sales verbs: unlock, supercharge, boost, accelerate (metaphorically), optimize, leverage
- Urgency verbs: hurry, act now, grab, seize, claim
- Competition verbs: outperform, beat, dominate, lead (as in market leadership)
- Hype verbs: blow away, crush it, nail it, smash

### Adjectives: What Modifies What

**Adjectives they use:**
- Scope: universal, all, every, global, fundamental
- Quality: world-class, curated, scholarly, free, open
- Scale (factual): largest, most complex (when literally true)
- Time: early, perpetual, forever, ages

**Adjectives they never use:**
- Marketing intensifiers: incredible, amazing, game-changing, seamless, robust
- Novelty: next-generation, cutting-edge, state-of-the-art, modern
- Emotional manipulation: exciting, thrilling, exclusive, premium
- Comparative: better, faster, smarter, easier (as selling points)

## Part III: The Seven Principles of Institutional Voice

### Principle 1: STATE, DO NOT SELL

These institutions declare what they are. They do not pitch, persuade, or convince. The Internet Archive says "Universal Access to All Knowledge" -- a statement so absolute it leaves no room for a call-to-action. Wikipedia says "the free encyclopedia that anyone can edit" -- a definition, not a value proposition.

**The rule:** Write as if the reader already agrees that your mission matters. Never argue for your own importance. If you have to explain why you matter, your copy has already failed.

**SaaS copy:** "The world's most powerful knowledge platform, trusted by millions."
**Institutional copy:** "A free encyclopedia that anyone can edit."

### Principle 2: USE THE LANGUAGE OF PERMANENCE, NOT MOMENTUM

SaaS copy obsesses over growth, speed, and the future. Institutional copy speaks in the language of deep time: "in perpetuity," "built to last the ages," "as long as civilization," "forever." These phrases anchor the project in something larger than quarterly goals or product roadmaps.

**The rule:** Position your work on the timescale of civilization, not the timescale of a product cycle. Use words like "endure," "preserve," "perpetuity," "ages." Avoid words like "fast-growing," "rapidly evolving," "next-generation."

**SaaS copy:** "Join the fastest-growing knowledge platform."
**Institutional copy:** "Free to the public, built to last the ages."

### Principle 3: LET THE SUBJECT CARRY THE GRANDEUR

CERN does not say "we do incredible work." It says "we probe the fundamental structure of particles that make up everything around us." The grandeur is in the subject matter -- the universe, the particles, the infinitely small. The language is plain; the content is extraordinary.

**The rule:** Never use adjectives to inject importance. Choose subjects and objects that are inherently significant. "Knowledge," "the universe," "all the published works of humankind," "civilization" -- these words carry their own weight. The copy just needs to point at them clearly.

**SaaS copy:** "Our revolutionary platform transforms how teams collaborate."
**Institutional copy:** "We probe the fundamental structure of particles that make up everything around us."

### Principle 4: PREFER THE CONCRETE AND SPECIFIC TO THE ABSTRACT

Project Gutenberg says "Alice in Wonderland, the Bible, Shakespeare" rather than "the world's literary heritage." arXiv says "two million scholarly articles in eight subject areas" rather than "a vast repository of academic knowledge." CERN says "close to the speed of light" rather than "at extraordinary velocities."

**The rule:** Name the specific thing. Give the number. Use the proper noun. Abstraction is the refuge of copy that has nothing real to say. Specificity is confidence.

**SaaS copy:** "Access a wealth of knowledge resources."
**Institutional copy:** "An online library of more than 75,000 free eBooks."

### Principle 5: USE SIMPLE, ANCIENT WORDS FOR YOUR BIGGEST IDEAS

The most powerful phrases in this research use elementary vocabulary: "all knowledge," "free to the public," "what the universe is made of," "how it works," "anyone can edit," "as long as civilization." These institutions reach for monosyllabic, Anglo-Saxon-root words when making their grandest claims. The simplicity of the language is proportional to the ambition of the idea.

**The rule:** The bigger the idea, the smaller the words. "Universal access to all knowledge" uses no word longer than three syllables. "Synergistic paradigm-shifting knowledge democratization framework" uses no word shorter than three syllables. The former is a mission statement for the ages. The latter is a PowerPoint slide.

**SaaS copy:** "Democratizing access to transformative knowledge ecosystems."
**Institutional copy:** "Universal access to all knowledge."

### Principle 6: ACKNOWLEDGE LIMITATIONS WITH QUIET HONESTY

arXiv states plainly: "Material is not peer-reviewed by arXiv." Project Gutenberg explains that they use "Plain Vanilla ASCII" because it works on 99% of systems -- acknowledging that this is a trade-off. Wikipedia's "that anyone can edit" is simultaneously its greatest strength and its most commonly cited vulnerability, and they present it without defensiveness.

**The rule:** State your limitations as facts, not disclaimers. Do not hedge, apologize, or spin. Honest acknowledgment of boundaries is more authoritative than the pretense of perfection. Every institution that claims to have no weaknesses sounds like a liar.

**SaaS copy:** (limitations are buried in footnotes or framed as "areas of active development")
**Institutional copy:** "Material is not peer-reviewed by arXiv."

### Principle 7: FRAME YOUR FOUNDING AS HISTORICAL FACT, NOT ORIGIN MYTHOLOGY

"arXiv was founded by Paul Ginsparg in 1991." "Michael Hart invented eBooks in 1971." "Founded in 1954, a handful of visionary scientists imagined creating a European atomic physics laboratory." These are sentences from history books, not from "Our Story" pages. The founders are named and dated. The achievements are stated without fanfare.

**The rule:** Describe your founding the way an encyclopedia would describe it fifty years from now. Use past tense. State the year. Name the person. Describe what they did in one verb. Do not use the word "journey." Do not use the word "passion." Do not describe anyone as a "serial entrepreneur."

**SaaS copy:** "Founded by two passionate entrepreneurs who saw a gap in the market..."
**Institutional copy:** "Michael Hart invented eBooks in 1971."

## Part IV: Sentence Templates

The following patterns recur across all five institutions. A copywriter can use these as structural templates:

### The Definition Statement
**Pattern:** "[Name] is [article] [plain noun] [prepositional phrase of scope]."
- "Wikipedia is a free encyclopedia that anyone can edit."
- "arXiv is a curated research-sharing platform open to anyone."
- "Project Gutenberg is an online library of more than 75,000 free eBooks."

### The Mission Declaration
**Pattern:** "[Totalizing phrase] [of/to] [all/every] [knowledge/people]."
- "Universal access to all knowledge."
- "Free access to the sum of all human knowledge."
- "For the benefit of all."

### The Founding Fact
**Pattern:** "[Person] [past-tense verb] [thing] in [year]."
- "Michael Hart invented eBooks in 1971."
- "arXiv was founded by Paul Ginsparg in 1991."
- "Founded in 1954..."

### The Method Statement
**Pattern:** "We [active verb] [the specific thing] [using/by/through] [the specific means]."
- "We probe the fundamental structure of particles that make up everything around us. We do so using the world's largest and most complex scientific instruments."

### The Permanence Pledge
**Pattern:** "[Available/free/open] [in perpetuity / forever / as long as civilization / to last the ages]."
- "Available on the internet free of charge, in perpetuity."
- "Free to the public, built to last the ages."
- "Will be with us as long as civilization."

### The Scope Inventory
**Pattern:** "[Number] [things] in [number] [categories]."
- "More than two million scholarly articles in eight subject areas."
- "More than 75,000 free eBooks."
- "25 member states."

## Part V: Words to Use and Words to Avoid

### USE FREELY
knowledge, access, free, open, public, preserve, curate, share, all, every, universal, fundamental, library, archive, repository, collection, works, scholarship, research, civilization, community, volunteer, contribute, perpetuity, endure

### USE SPARINGLY
world-class (only if literally true), pioneer (only as historical fact), unique (only if literally one-of-a-kind), visionary (once, at most, in a founding narrative)

### NEVER USE
revolutionary, game-changing, disruptive, next-generation, cutting-edge, seamless, robust, scalable, leverage, unlock, supercharge, reimagine, best-in-class, industry-leading, ecosystem (for a non-ecological system), journey (for a company history), passionate (for founders), stakeholders (for people), deliverables (for work), synergy, alignment, empower (in a SaaS context), democratize (as a buzzword)

## Part VI: The Underlying Philosophy

What unites all five institutions is a single rhetorical posture: **they serve something larger than themselves, and they know it.**

Wikipedia serves knowledge. The Internet Archive serves memory. CERN serves understanding. arXiv serves scholarship. Project Gutenberg serves literature. None of them serve "users" or "customers." None of them exist to "solve pain points."

This orientation toward a transcendent purpose -- knowledge, truth, memory, understanding -- is what makes their language feel noble. The copy is not humble (these are ambitious projects), but it is selfless. The institution is always the instrument, never the subject of admiration.

The practical implication for a copywriter: **write about the thing you serve, not about yourself.** Describe knowledge, not your platform. Describe preservation, not your features. Describe access, not your interface. The institution disappears into its mission, and that disappearance is what creates the sense of nobility.

A SaaS company says: "We built an amazing platform."
A knowledge institution says: "All the published works of humankind, free to the public, built to last the ages."

In the first sentence, the company is the subject. In the second, the works of humankind are the subject. That shift in grammatical subject is the entire difference between marketing and majesty.
